When should a computer work?

Electricity is not equally clean around the clock. Give a computation the freedom to pick its hour, and it can shed a quarter of its carbon — predictably, reliably, for free. A data story from the German power grid.

The grid breathes

On 14 July 2025, Germany’s electricity was not one product but many. At six in the evening, with solar fading and demand peaking, a kilowatt-hour carried 546 g of CO₂. At noon, with photovoltaics flooding the grid, the same kilowatt-hour carried 221 g — 60% less.

Most electricity users can’t choose their hour. A computer often can. Training runs, batch inference, data pipelines — they need hours of power, but rarely these particular hours. Drag the job below and watch its footprint change.

The cheapest climate action a computer can take is to look at the clock.

Not one lucky day

That July day is not an outlier. Across 2024–2025, the median German day offered a 175 gCO₂/kWh gap between its cleanest and dirtiest four-hour window — the clean one landing between 09:00 and 14:00 on 72% of days, when solar output peaks. The pattern is strongly seasonal: deep, reliable midday valleys in summer, flatter and windier days in winter.

Mean carbon intensity of German electricity by hour and month, 2024–2025. Computed from ENTSO-E generation data.

Can you see it coming?

A schedule is only as good as its forecast: at midnight you must commit tomorrow’s plan. We trained a gradient-boosted model on 2024 to predict hourly carbon intensity up to 36 hours ahead, then tested it on all of 2025 — never letting it see information that wasn’t public at forecast time.

The decisive ingredient is not the model. It is Germany’s day-ahead wind and solar forecasts, published daily by grid operators. With them, mid-range error drops by more than half; the carbon intensity of a solar-heavy grid is, to a large degree, a weather forecast wearing an emissions factor.

Mean absolute error of day-ahead CI forecasts by horizon, test year 2025. Lower is better.

Overall the model beats a seasonal-naïve baseline by 42% — and in the window that matters for scheduling (the next 24 hours), its error is 31 g/kWh against the baseline’s 76.

The experiment

So: one four-hour training job at five kilowatts, every day of 2025, free to start at any hour. Three schedulers compete. One picks a random hour — the computer that doesn’t look at the clock. One follows our forecast, committed the night before. One cheats: perfect knowledge of the future, the physical ceiling.

Total 2025 emissions of the daily 4-hour job under three scheduling policies, DE grid.

The forecast scheduler cut emissions by 28%. The omniscient one managed 29%. In other words, an ordinary public-data forecast captures 96% of the theoretical maximum — on a solar-shaped grid, tomorrow’s clean window is simply not that hard to see coming. Per job that is 629 kg of CO₂ a year, the footprint of driving 5,239 km. Across a fleet of a thousand such jobs: 629 tonnes.

Or just move?

Timing is not the only degree of freedom — batch compute can also change country. Great Britain’s grid, gas-and-wind shaped and free of lignite, ran cleaner than Germany’s on 99% of 2025 days.

Mean carbon intensity by month, 2024–2025: Germany vs Great Britain.

The same daily job, 2025 total: staying and timing vs moving. Green = Germany, blue = Great Britain.

Moving beats waiting: relocating the job to Britain saves more than perfect timing in Germany ever could. But the comparison is not symmetric. Changing when a job runs costs nothing — no data transfer, no residency questions, no second data centre. Changing where is an infrastructure decision. The honest reading: timing is the free lever, geography is the big one, and they compound — a timed job in Britain emits 71% less than a random one in Germany.

What this doesn’t fix

Carbon-aware scheduling optimises when, not how much. The job still burns the same kilowatt-hours; the grid still burns what it burns. Three honest limits:

  • Average, not marginal. We price each hour at the grid’s average intensity. The marginal plant answering one extra kilowatt is often gas regardless of hour, so system-level savings from small shifts are smaller than average-based accounting suggests. At fleet scale, though, demand that reliably chases clean hours changes what gets built — the long-run signal points the same way.
  • Production, not consumption. Our intensity ignores imports and exports; German midday solar partly flows abroad, and winter imports flow in.
  • Timing is a complement, not an alibi. The real decarbonisation is the grid’s own. Flexible demand just stops wasting the clean hours the grid already produces — a lever that is real, measurable, and free, but a lever, not an engine.

Methods

Carbon intensity is computed from ENTSO-E actual generation per production type for bidding zone DE-LU (15-minute resolution, 2024–2025), weighted by IPCC lifecycle emission factors per fuel, resampled hourly. The series correlates at r = 0.992 with the independent Energy-Charts (Fraunhofer ISE) reference; our conservative lignite factor gives a −74 g/kWh level offset that does not affect timing decisions. British data comes from the NESO Carbon Intensity API.

The forecaster is a LightGBM model over lagged CI, calendar features, and ENTSO-E day-ahead wind, solar, and load forecasts, trained on 2024, tested out-of-sample on 2025 from daily midnight origins, horizons 1–36 h. Day-ahead exogenous forecasts are only used for horizons where they were genuinely published. The scheduling simulation settles every policy against actual intensity. The job spec (4 h at 5 kW ≈ one 8-GPU node) is a scale anchor — percentages are independent of it.
